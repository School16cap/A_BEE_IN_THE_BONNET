Pylyaev wrote that " Catherine introduced at court the elegant simplicity of Russian dress." All were to appear at court in dresses with elements of the Russian national costume. Under Pavel Petrovich, this tradition was not observed. Alexander Pavlovich didn't care who was wearing what. Everyone dressed in the French fashion. But once, during the Patriotic War, Golitsyn, who is the "queen of spades", came to the ball dressed in Russian folk dress in protest. They say it made a splash. Unfortunately, no one has described exactly what the "mustachioed countess"was wearing. Sarafan, dushegreya, shugay? Brocade, damask, or silk? A cocoanut on his head, or maybe a magpie?

The traditional costume of the Nizhny Novgorod region is quite diverse. The population living along the Volga River and the lower Oka River wore costumes similar to those of the northern provinces. Majestic, massive, voluminous sarafan-kosoklinnik, forcing to straighten even the most stooped back, gold embroidered shawls and epanichki (dushegrei), women's and girls ' headdresses, studded with pearls-these are the characteristic features of the images of traditional women's clothing of this territory.All women's clothing can be divided into sets with a panel and sets with a sundress. Paneva is considered a more ancient type of women's clothing.

2nd question

distinctive features of traditional clothing in the Nizhny Novgorod province?

The traditional clothing of the inhabitants of the Nizhny Novgorod province was diverse. The costumes worn in its southern and northern parts were particularly prominent. A common item of folk clothing was a shirt. The female version of it was sewn, as a rule, up to the middle of the shin or to the feet, from homespun canvas. The sleeves, chest, and hem were richly decorated with embroidery, patterned weaving, ribbons, and braid. The necks of Nizhny Novgorod shirts were decorated with a figured assembly. The edges of the sleeves were also made the same.

A girl's holiday costume. XIX century. Nizhny Novgorod province On the left: a bandage, a shirt, a shower jacket, a necklace. On the right: armband, sundress.

The silk sarafan was accompanied by a short, open-breasted garment with straps, which was called epanechka in the Nizhny Novgorod province. It was considered a kind of dushegrei, sewn with a lining, quilted on cotton wool or tow, from expensive purchased materials — silk, velvet, corduroy, brocade — and decorated with strips of braid, fringe of metal thread and fur fringe. On the back, the epanechka was collected in large tubular folds-burs. In different counties, it was called kholodnik or kaftanchik and was known even in the XVI—XVII centuries, then it was worn by women and girls only from boyar and merchant families. Later, the epanechka became a detail of the city costume of merchants and rich middle-class women. In the peasant environment, it was quite rare, mainly in rich families who lived in suburban, commercial and artisan villages. The popularity of epanechka among women of the Nizhny Novgorod province is explained by the trade and profitable trades developed in this region. Over the shirt, women wore a sundress — one of the most common items of women's clothing in Russia. In the northern regions of the Nizhny Novgorod province, its old type was long worn — the kosoklinny swing, popular among women of the Old Believer villages (their sarafans were black or blue, they were worn complete with a white shirt with lace cuffs). Festive versions of it in the XVIII century were made of expensive silks and decorated with a long row of puffed buttons on the front. Later, sundresses were no longer made swing, and instead of buttons, they were sewn with colored braid, imitating a clasp.
